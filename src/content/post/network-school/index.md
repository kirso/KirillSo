I broke my ankle a week before flying to Malaysia. Torn ligaments, the whole thing. Not exactly the "lock in and grind" energy I had planned. But I went anyway, hobbling through security with a boot on my foot and a suitcase full of optimism.

Network School is Balaji Srinivasan's project — a physical community where people live, work, and work out together. Think of it as a co-living space for builders, with structured meals, a gym, coworking, events, and a heavy crypto-adjacent identity. I was there for the February 2026 batch, the largest one yet with around 300 people.

---

## Why I went

The pitch is simple: food, gym, cowork — all at your fingertips. No decision fatigue. No hunting for a decent cafe with Wi-Fi. You show up, and the infrastructure handles the rest.

That's what drew me in. I was deep into building [Keepsake](https://www.makekeepsake.com) and I needed consistency, not adventure. I also invited two friends to come along so we could hold each other accountable. The idea was to lock in, ship work, and be around people who are doing the same.

## Arriving

When I got there, the staff was genuinely welcoming. They handed me a box with swag and instructions, I checked in, went for lunch, and that was it. No friction. The onboarding was smooth and I appreciated not having to figure things out on my own with a busted ankle.

The morning routine became my favorite part — coffee, a slow walk to the coworking space, catching up with people along the way. There's something about having a default environment where everyone is building something that makes even small talk feel more energizing than usual.

## The people

Here's the thing nobody tells you about Network School: it's not a crypto bro convention.

I went in half-expecting wall-to-wall DeFi evangelists. What I found was a genuinely diverse group. A lawyer who came with his wife and newborn because he'd read about the network state concept. A man from Atlanta working on accessibility who was just an incredibly kind person to be around. A young applied AI researcher building agent orchestration tools. An architect exploring a product in her niche. Families with kids.

Everyone was nice. I didn't have a single weird or bad encounter, which is saying something when you put 300 strangers in close proximity for a month.

That said — and I want to be honest about this — some people felt a bit floaty. Not serious in a way that's hard to articulate. I think it's because many were in exploration mode, still researching what they wanted to do. I recognized it because I spent years in that exact state, endlessly brainstorming ideas instead of building. That phase produces a lot of TARPIT ideas and "yet another AI B2B lead generation SaaS" pitches as if the world needed more of those.

So I kept my distance from the brainstorming crowd and gravitated towards founders and builders who were already doing the work. That's not a judgment — everyone's on their own timeline. But when you come to lock in, you want to be around people who are locked in too.

## Events and Balaji's lunches

There are events happening all the time. Sports, men's circle, setting up OpenClaw, technical talks — you could fill every hour if you wanted to. I didn't. I was there to work, not to optimize my social calendar.

But I did attend the weekly Lunch with Balaji, which was a highlight. He'd outline his vision for Network School, talk about where things are heading. One week there was a session on how LLM models are being fine-tuned. Another time, a professor from a US university gave a talk on embryo selection that gave me strong Gattaca vibes. I still need to watch that movie.

The Ethereum Foundation also ran an L2 meetup during our batch, which brought in another wave of people. It added to the energy, though it also made the place feel crowded.

## The food situation

The food is genuinely great. Hotel breakfast in the morning, a lunch box during the day, and a dinner buffet. There's also a cafe downstairs for the in-between hours. Quality-wise, no complaints.

The problem was logistics. With 300 people in the batch, I'd regularly show up to dinner and find empty trays. They'd refill them, so the wait was never catastrophic, but it happened every single day. The kind of minor annoyance that compounds over a month.

## The fitness evaluation

Given my ankle situation, I couldn't do much in the way of sports. No tennis on the beach, no badminton, none of the outdoor activities that people raved about.

What I did do was the fitness evaluation — pushups, pullups, grip strength, cardio, body measurements, coordination (standing on one leg, which was entertaining given my condition). It was actually fun. The kind of structured assessment I'd never bother setting up for myself but appreciated having access to.

As for the gym itself, it's decent. I was a bit bummed there's no smith machine or T-bar row, which are staples in my routine. Not a dealbreaker, but worth knowing if you care about specific equipment.

## A few things that could be better

**Accessibility is a real problem.** The coworking space is not accessible, and many events are held there. If someone with a mobility issue shows up — which, as I learned firsthand with my ankle — getting around without help is borderline impossible. Same goes for the bus transport to Forest City. This should be a priority to fix.

**The referral situation left a sour taste.** I referred two friends who paid full price. I didn't use an official referral link because I didn't know one existed. When I asked if the credits could be applied retroactively, NS said no. It's a small thing, but it felt unnecessarily rigid for something that should be easy to resolve. It killed my motivation to refer anyone else.

## The crypto elephant in the room

For some reason, people think Network School is a scam, a pyramid scheme, or some kind of cult. The reality couldn't be further from the truth. Blockchain and crypto are part of NS's identity, sure. But it wasn't something shoved in my face daily. I'm not even into crypto anymore. I came to work and be around builders, because there aren't a lot of hackerspaces like this in Southeast Asia.

Is there a crypto lean? Yes. Are there people raising money for crypto projects? Absolutely. But there are also digital nomads, freelancers, families, and people who just want a structured environment to get things done.

## Would I recommend it?

Yes, with caveats.

If you're in building mode and want an environment that removes friction — meals, gym, workspace, all handled — Network School delivers on that promise. If you want to explore, meet interesting people, or even raise money for a crypto project, it's a solid bet. NS is also rolling out long-term offerings and a fellowship with investment in your company, which could be interesting for the right founder.

But if you're going to be productive, you'll need to protect your time. Conversations at lunch and dinner can easily stretch to two or three hours. The social pull of 300 people is real, and if you're not intentional about your schedule, the days will slip away in pleasant but unproductive conversation.

Go with people you know. Have a project you're actively shipping. Treat it as a work retreat with perks, not a social experiment, and you'll get a lot out of it.
